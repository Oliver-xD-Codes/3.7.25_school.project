The Civil Rights Act of 1964

Introduction

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was a landmark piece of legislation in the United States that outlawed discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. It ended segregation in public places and banned employment discrimination.

Background

Prior to the Act, racial segregation and discrimination were widespread, particularly in the southern United States. African Americans faced barriers in education, employment, and public services. The Civil Rights Movement, led by figures such as Martin Luther King Jr. and Rosa Parks, pushed for legislative change.

Key Provisions

  • Title II: Prohibited discrimination in public accommodations (hotels, restaurants, theaters, etc.).
  • Title III: Encouraged desegregation of public facilities.
  • Title IV: Promoted desegregation in public schools.
  • Title VII: Banned employment discrimination and established the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC).

Impact

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 paved the way for future legislation, including the Voting Rights Act of 1965. It was a major step toward equality and helped shape modern civil rights protections. The Act had a lasting impact on society and laid the foundation for further civil rights advancements.
